Your rights after a hospital death

A practical guide for families in Scotland who have lost a relative in hospital and are being stonewalled when they try to find out what happened.

I work in technology governance and data rights. For thirty years my job has been to understand how large organisations hold information, how they decide who gets to see it, and what the law actually requires of them. This guide is that expertise applied to a problem I did not choose.

My mother died in a Scottish hospital. I spent twenty months trying to obtain her records and a straight account of her care. I was passed between departments, asked for documents the law does not require, and told I did not qualify when I did. I learned, slowly and at cost, that families have far more rights than hospitals tend to admit, and that most refusals do not survive contact with the actual legislation.

None of this should have to be learned alone, in grief, while a public body runs down the clock. So I have written down what I worked out. Each guide below covers one task: how to get the records, what to do when you are refused, who regulates whom, and the rights you hold as a relative. It is plain English, it is free, and it is built to be used.

The guides

  • 01Request a deceased relative's medical recordsTwo legal routes, who to write to, and a template letter. No solicitor, no fee.
  • 02What to do when the hospital refusesCommon refusal tactics, why they are usually wrong, and how to apply to the Sheriff Court.
  • 03How to complain to the right regulatorGMC, NMC, SSSC, SPSO, ICO, SLCC: who handles what, and how to reach them.
  • 04How to complain to the ICOWhen records are withheld or a data request is ignored.
  • 05How to complain to the SPSOWhen the NHS complaints procedure has not put things right.
  • 06Your rights as a child of the deceasedLegal rights in moveable estate, the 20-year window, and acting as executor.
  • 07Request your own data from the hospitalA Subject Access Request for what they hold about you. Template letter included.
  • 08Free and low-cost helpAdvocacy, specialist charities, legal aid and party litigant guides.
This is practical guidance based on personal experience. It is not legal advice. If you are unsure about your situation, seek advice from a solicitor or Citizens Advice.
