How to complain to the right regulator

A complaint sent to the wrong body wastes months. This is who handles what.

What is this?

There is no single regulator for everything that can go wrong around a hospital death. Different bodies cover doctors, nurses, social care workers, the data law, complaint handling, and solicitors. This guide matches the problem to the body, and tells you what each one can and cannot do.

A useful rule of thumb: regulators of individuals (GMC, NMC, SSSC) look at a named professional's fitness to practise. Regulators of organisations and process (SPSO, ICO) look at how a body behaved. They are not alternatives to each other. You can pursue more than one in parallel.

GMC: General Medical Council

Who it covers: doctors.

What it investigates: serious or repeated concerns about a doctor's fitness to practise, including clinical failings and professional misconduct. It is not a route to compensation, and it does not resolve individual disputes about treatment; it acts where a doctor's ability to practise safely is in question.

How to submit: through the GMC's online complaint form at gmc-uk.org.

Timescales and outcomes: investigations can take many months. Outcomes range from no action, through warnings and conditions, to suspension or removal from the register.

The GMC's own guidance, Treatment and care towards the end of life, is important context if your concern is about end-of-life decisions. It states that doctors must start from a presumption in favour of prolonging life (paragraph 7), must presume that an adult patient has capacity to make decisions (paragraph 8), and that a competent patient is entitled to decide between the treatment options offered (paragraph 10). If what happened departed from these principles, quote them.

NMC: Nursing and Midwifery Council

Who it covers: nurses and midwives.

What it investigates: concerns about a nurse's or midwife's fitness to practise.

How to submit: through the referral process at nmc.org.uk.

Timescales and outcomes: investigations commonly take many months. Outcomes include warnings, conditions of practice, suspension or removal from the register.

SSSC: Scottish Social Services Council

Who it covers: social care workers registered in Scotland.

What it investigates: the conduct and fitness to practise of registered social service workers.

How to submit: through the concerns process at sssc.uk.com.

Timescales and outcomes: variable; outcomes range from no action to removal from the register.

SPSO: Scottish Public Services Ombudsman

Who it covers: the NHS board, as an organisation.

What it investigates: how the board handled your complaint, and the standard of clinical care and treatment. You must normally complete the NHS complaints procedure first.

How to submit: online at spso.org.uk.

Timescales and outcomes: see the dedicated SPSO guide. Outcomes are recommendations and published findings, not compensation.

ICO: Information Commissioner's Office

Who it covers: any organisation handling personal data, including the NHS board.

What it investigates: data protection failings, such as ignoring a Subject Access Request, withholding records you are entitled to, or mishandling personal data.

How to submit: online at ico.org.uk.

Timescales and outcomes: see the dedicated ICO guide. The ICO can issue reprimands and enforcement notices but cannot award compensation.

SLCC: Scottish Legal Complaints Commission

Who it covers: solicitors in Scotland.

What it investigates: complaints about the service or conduct of a solicitor, for example if a solicitor you instructed let the matter drift.

How to submit: through the complaints process at scottishlegalcomplaints.org.uk.

Timescales and outcomes: the SLCC can uphold service complaints and direct redress, and refers conduct issues to the Law Society of Scotland.
